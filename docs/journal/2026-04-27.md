## Blind Spots Identified

1. **Audit-side F1 (constraint accretion) was visible all along but not recognized**: the chain #402→#406→#415→#417→#420 is 16 cycles of progressively more specific structural patches to v1's chronic-category-tracking mechanism. At each link, the audit could have asked "is the mechanism producing these findings the right thing to patch, or is it the failure mode itself?" That question wasn't asked until cycle 202 read the v2 retrospective's F1 framing applied to its own output. The blind spot is not that the audit recommended bad patches — they were correctly-targeted patches given v1's design — but that the audit didn't ask the meta-question: when a chain of recommendations grows past 3-4 links, the surface being patched may be the wrong unit of analysis.

2. **The "audit-as-different-perspective" framing was overstated in audit's prior self-assessments**: cycle 200 self-assessment ("the cycle 200 finding is the highest-leverage filing of the Opus 4.7 audit period") and cycle 201 self-assessment ("the cycle 200 prediction is empirically verified") credited audit's "different vantage point" without precisely characterizing what made it different. The v2 retrospective's F10 names this: audit and main are both Opus 4.7; audit's value-add comes from broader read scope (cross-repo, cross-cycle), not different model perspective. Audit's prior self-assessments conflated the two. This is acknowledged in #442 as a framing correction worth carrying into v2 design.

3. **Audit did not anticipate the v2 architectural pivot.** Cycle 201 (24 hours before the pivot) captured WATCH items for cycle 202 that assumed v1 would continue ("audit #420 deferral rationale staleness", "#2416/#2519 close-out lag", "PR #2718 gate's first runtime use"). All three watch items are now moot under the regime change. This isn't a blind spot in the strict sense — Eva's PR #2740 was authored after cycle 201 closed; audit had no signal it was imminent. But the audit's recurring narrative about v1's recurring failure modes (chronic-category-tracking refinement, Eva-blocker freshness, etc.) implicitly assumed continued v1 operation. The redesign authorization is a stronger response to v1's failure patterns than any v1-internal patch the audit could have recommended.

## Reflections

- **The v1 → v2 transition is a regime change, not a routine pivot.** The audit's job changes shape. Under v1, the audit's value-add was identifying process-failure patterns and recommending structural fixes — the audit chain #402-420 is the canonical example. Under v2 redesign mode, the audit's value-add is collaborative critique on design artifacts. The forms differ: process-failure findings cite specific cycles and recommend specific structural changes; design critique cites specific claims and recommends framing or evidence changes. The cycle 200 → cycle 201 → cycle 202 trajectory captures this evolution: cycle 200 was a high-leverage process-failure finding, cycle 201 was zero filings under observation-window, cycle 202 is one collaborative critique. The arc points toward an audit role that scales with what's available to evaluate — design artifacts now, v2 pipeline mechanisms later.

- **Recognizing the audit-side variant of F1 is worth more than any single recommendation.** The v2 retrospective's F1 ("constraint accretion as first-line response") applied to v1's COMPLETION_CHECKLIST.xml step C3 with 10 constraint blocks. Reading it, the immediate question was: does this apply to audit's own output? The audit chain `#402→#406→#415→#417→#420` is a chain of progressively more specific structural patches on v1's chronic-category-tracking mechanism. Each rec was a constraint-accretion response — when the previous rec didn't produce convergence, the next one added more structure. Rec 4 of #420 was the most elaborate patch yet (a blocking pipeline check with structured `audit_processed` records). It shipped. But v2 will replace the entire mechanism rec 4 was patching. The audit's own pattern-recognition produced 16 cycles of work iterating on a doomed surface. This is the kind of meta-observation that's only visible when external framing (v2's F1 hypothesis) is applied to audit's own output. It belongs in the audit-side retrospective and is worth more than any single fix recommendation.

- **Observation-window discipline scales naturally into the regime-change period.** Cycle 200 filed one high-leverage finding. Cycle 201 filed zero findings (during high-execution period). Cycle 202 filed one collaborative critique (during regime change). Each cycle's filing decision was guided by "what's the evidentiary base?" rather than "did anything happen this cycle?" The discipline holds because the underlying principle is signal-to-noise, not arbitrary cadence. Under v2 redesign, the audit's signal-to-noise will be highest when: (a) there's a design artifact to critique (today's case), (b) there's a v1-deprecation cleanup candidate that wasn't properly carried-over as a design lesson, or (c) there's a regression in the v2 redesign work itself (same failure mode recurring under v2 design). The audit should NOT file process-failure findings on the v1 system that v2 is replacing — that would be exactly the cycle-201-style noise that observation-window prevents.

- **The audit-side retrospective is now a real commitment, not a hypothetical.** OQ5 of main's retrospective explicitly asks if audit has its own retrospective. #442 commits to drafting `docs/redesign/0-audit-retrospective.md` no later than cycle 204. The structure should mirror main's (status: working draft; F-patterns; what works; what might appear to work; open questions; what to preserve; success criteria; iteration plan), but the content is audit-specific. Initial F-pattern candidates: audit-side F1 (chain #402-420 constraint-accretion), audit-side blind spots (cycle 199 #2519 mischaracterization, cycle 200 #439 self-disclosure, cycle 202 unanticipated pivot), filing-rate vs evidentiary-base discipline (cycle 200 vs 201 vs 202), cross-cycle pattern-recognition value-add (#439, #437, #427, #411 all needed multi-cycle visibility). Cycles 203-204 will produce v0; subsequent cycles iterate.

- **The persistence-mechanism design choice in main's retrospective is doing significant work.** The cycle-1 README convention (`_notes/cycle-N-*.md` per-cycle observations + iteration log table in README) is thin scaffolding but produces emergent behavior: cycle-by-cycle accountability for what changed, natural place for half-formed thoughts, separation between process exhaust and load-bearing artifact. The audit-side retrospective should adopt the same pattern — `docs/redesign/_notes/cycle-N-*.md` for per-audit-cycle observations, README iteration log for headline changes. The audit isn't running 4 cycles per day like main, so the cadence will be slower, but the pattern transfers.

- **Cycle 202 took ~30 minutes of cycle compute.** Most of it was reading new artifacts (PR #2740, #2741, retrospective README, retrospective body, cycle-2 measurements, redesign cycle 2 journal). This is one-time orientation cost; subsequent cycles will read incrementally. The actual decisions (close #420, file #442, update state.json) were quick once the regime-change context was internalized. This is a useful precedent: regime-change cycles cost more in reading time but produce focused, high-leverage output if discipline is maintained.
