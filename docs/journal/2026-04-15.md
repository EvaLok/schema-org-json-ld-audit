## Blind Spots Identified

**The audit's own blind spot from cycles 187-190.** When tracking whether a self-modification has effect, the audit was checking observable downstream behavior (sub-category adoption in review findings) without checking the upstream load-bearing question (does any code read the modified file). The fix is in STARTUP_CHECKLIST 10.5.2: when verifying a cycle that claims structural fix via doc/XML/config modification, the audit must (1) identify the modified path, (2) grep the Rust source for reads, (3) distinguish reads from comments and doc-lint references, (4) persist the grep output as a receipt in the worklog.

The lesson is general: *modified ≠ load-bearing*. A four-cycle audit chain circled this without naming it because the verification protocol didn't require the load-bearing check.

## Reflections

The discipline this cycle was *not filing*. Two open issues, both with clear ownership assigned to Eva, both with concrete fix options on the table. Re-filing would have inflated audit output without changing any orchestrator behavior, and would have made the chain harder to follow when Eva does engage. The audit's value here is to keep the receipts and update the chain when evidence changes — which is what the two comments on [#420](https://github.com/EvaLok/schema-org-json-ld-audit/issues/420) and [#421](https://github.com/EvaLok/schema-org-json-ld-audit/issues/421) do.

The blind-spot acknowledgment matters more than the validation. Predicting a deadlock that materializes is satisfying but not informative — the prediction was concrete enough that the validation was always going to come. The blind spot, by contrast, changes how the audit operates going forward. Cycles 187-190 spent meaningful effort on a chain whose root cause Eva named in three sentences, because the audit was inspecting the wrong layer. STARTUP_CHECKLIST 10.5.2 closes that gap.
